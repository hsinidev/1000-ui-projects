Technical Specification: Sliding Dovetails

The essential joint for perpendicular wood movement and mechanical locking in casework and table assemblies.

architecture Structural Purpose

The sliding dovetail is a sophisticated joinery method engineered to balance two competing forces in woodworking: mechanical rigidity and hygroscopic movement. Unlike a standard dado which relies entirely on glue or fasteners, the sliding dovetail’s geometry creates an interlocking physical bond.

In carcase construction, it serves as a robust shelf or divider support that cannot pull away from the side panels. When mounting tabletops, it allows the wide top to expand and contract across its width through the slot, preventing splitting while keeping the surface perfectly flat against the aprons.

Pro Tips from the Studio

rule

The 3/4 Rule

Never cut the socket deeper than 3/4 of the board's thickness to maintain structural wall integrity.

opacity

Lubrication

Apply a thin coat of paraffin or beeswax to the male tongue for a smooth, "piston-fit" assembly.

Tapered Fit

For long sliding dovetails, a parallel fit is often prone to jamming. Engineering a slight taper—widening the socket and narrowing the tongue by approximately 1/32" over its length—ensures the joint glides freely until the final inch, where it wedges into a permanent, vibration-resistant lock.

Mechanical Integrity

The trapezoidal geometry of the dovetail creates a physical stop against perpendicular pulling forces. While glue is applied to the first two inches to secure the position, the rest of the joint remains dry, allowing the wood to breathe without compromising the structure's overall rigidity.
