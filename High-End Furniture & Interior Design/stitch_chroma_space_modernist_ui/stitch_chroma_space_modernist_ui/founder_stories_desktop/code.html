In a world of fluid boundaries and digital vapor, Elias Vance remains a radical materialist. For over a decade, his work has challenged the very notion of 'soft' design, favoring the brutal, the permanent, and the honest.

THE ANATOMY OF STRUCTURE

"Most people are afraid of edges. They want everything to be rounded, safe, and approachable. But safety is the enemy of truth in design. When we remove the border, we remove the definition. A chair that tries to disappear isn't a chair; it's a failure of intent."

Vance sits in his studio—a converted Brutalist warehouse in Berlin—surrounded by prototypes that look more like industrial machinery than domestic furniture. His latest series, 'Chroma-Lith', uses primary yellows and raw steel to create a visual language that screams for attention.

"The border is the only thing keeping the world from dissolving..."
QUESTION

Why color? Why now?

ELIAS VANCE

Color isn't decoration. It's information. In a chaotic environment, a primary yellow border is a navigation system. It tells the eye where to go, what to touch, and where the boundaries are. We've used beige to hide for too long.

ELIAS VANCE

The 'Chroma-Space' project was born out of a desire to create a physical manifesto. It’s not just a shop; it’s an ecosystem for people who aren't afraid of high-energy living.
