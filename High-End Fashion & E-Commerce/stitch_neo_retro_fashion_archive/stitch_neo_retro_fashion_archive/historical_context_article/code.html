THE SILKEN REVOLUTION: EVOLUTION OF LUXURY TEXTILES

Exploring the intersection of craftsmanship, power, and the tactile history of the high-fashion archive.

Circa 1920

THE ART DECO WEAVE

In the early 20th century, luxury was defined by the weight of the weave. The Archival collections from the 1920s reveal a shift towards geometric metallic threads, mirroring the architectural rigidity of the era while maintaining a fluid, nocturnal drape.

Circa 1950

THE JURASSIC JACQUARD

Post-war exuberance led to the revitalization of heavy looms. This era introduced high-relief floral motifs that stood off the garment like architectural moldings. The Archive houses three distinct variants of the 'Golden Rose' weave, each representing a peak in French textile engineering.

1980 - PRESENT

THE MINIMALIST SYNTHESIS

By the 1980s, luxury turned inward. The tactility of the archive became more subtle, focusing on the microscopic tension of the thread rather than the overtness of the pattern. Designers like Yohji Yamamoto and Rei Kawakubo challenged the traditional "high-finish" of textiles, introducing deconstruction and raw edges into the archival canon.
