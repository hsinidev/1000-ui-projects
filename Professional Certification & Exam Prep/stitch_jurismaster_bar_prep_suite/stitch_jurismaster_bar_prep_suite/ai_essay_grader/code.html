The Doctrine of Promissory Estoppel

Student ID: JD-2024-8842 • Submitted: Oct 14, 2023

balance

In the matter of Central London Property Trust Ltd v High Trees House Ltd [1947] KB 130, the court established a foundational principle that would alter the landscape of contract law. Promissory estoppel serves as a shield, not a sword, providing a defense against the strict enforcement of legal rights when a party has made a clear representation intended to be relied upon.

The plaintiff, High Trees, sought to recover full rent for the period of the second world war despite an agreement to accept half-rent due to low occupancy. Denning J (as he then was) articulated that where a promise is made which is intended to create legal relations and which, to the knowledge of the person making the promise, was going to be acted on by the person to whom it was made and which was in fact so acted on, then the promise is binding.

The application of this doctrine requires three core elements: a clear and unequivocal promise, reliance on that promise by the promisee, and it being inequitable for the promisor to go back on their word. However, it is critical to note that promissory estoppel does not create new causes of action where none existed before. It only prevents a party from insisting on their strict legal rights when it would be unconscionable to do so.

In contemporary legal practice, the rigors of this doctrine ensure that contractual fairness is maintained without subverting the necessity of consideration. The evolution of the rule suggests a shift toward equitable outcomes in commercial disputes.
