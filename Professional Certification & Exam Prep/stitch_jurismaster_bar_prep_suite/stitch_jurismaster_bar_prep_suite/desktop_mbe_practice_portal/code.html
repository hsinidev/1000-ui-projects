Constitutional Law: The Commerce Clause

A state legislature recently enacted a statute requiring all commercial trucks traveling on state highways to be equipped with a specific type of contoured rear-fender mudguard. The statute's stated purpose is to reduce the spray of water and mud on trailing vehicles during rainstorms, thereby improving visibility and road safety.

An out-of-state trucking company, which operates a fleet of several hundred trucks throughout the region, challenges the statute. The company provides evidence that the required contoured mudguards are not significantly safer than the conventional straight mudguards that are legal in the 47 other states where the company operates. Furthermore, the company shows that installing these specific mudguards on its entire fleet would cost over $500,000 and that changing them at the state line for every transit would be functionally impossible.

Under the Dormant Commerce Clause, which of the following is the most likely outcome of the trucking company's challenge?

Question FAQ

Doesn't "Police Power" allow for safety laws?

While states have broad police power, the **Dormant Commerce Clause** limits laws that place an "undue burden" on interstate commerce, even if they aren't discriminatory on their face.

What is the "Pike Balancing Test"?

Derived from Pike v. Bruce Church, Inc., it weighs the state's interest against the burden on commerce. If the burden is clearly excessive in relation to the local benefits, the law is unconstitutional.

Is this related to Preemption?

Not necessarily. Preemption applies if a federal law exists. If Congress hasn't acted, we look to the "Dormant" status of the Commerce Clause to see if the state action is prohibited.

Pro-Tip

Look for "contradictory" state laws. If every state required different mudguards, interstate commerce would grind to a halt—a classic Dormant Commerce Clause violation.
