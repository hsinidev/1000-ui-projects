Clinical Presentation

A 54-year-old male with a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and a 40 pack-year smoking history presents to the emergency department with a 3-day history of worsening shortness of breath and a productive cough with yellow-green sputum. On physical examination, he is tachypneic with a respiratory rate of 28/min and oxygen saturation of 88% on room air.

Auscultation reveals diffuse wheezing and distant breath sounds. Arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis on room air shows pH 7.32, PaCO2 52 mmHg, and PaO2 55 mmHg. A chest X-ray shows hyperinflated lungs and a flattened diaphragm with no acute infiltrates.

Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in the management of this patient's acute exacerbation?

Great mnemonic for NIPPV indications in COPD:

"CARB"

  • Confusion (Mental status shift)
  • Acidosis (pH < 7.35)
  • Respiratory Distress (RR > 25)
  • Breathlessness (Severe)
Dr_InternalMed • 6h ago

Note the ABG: pH 7.32 and PaCO2 52. This is respiratory acidosis with hypercapnia. NIPPV is the gold standard for acute COPD exacerbations with respiratory acidosis (pH < 7.35 or PaCO2 > 45) before jumping to intubation.
