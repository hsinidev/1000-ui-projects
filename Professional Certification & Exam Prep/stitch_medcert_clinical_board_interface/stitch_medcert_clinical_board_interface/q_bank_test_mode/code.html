A 54-year-old male presents to the emergency department with a 3-hour history of severe, substernal chest pain that radiates to his left jaw and shoulder. The pain began while he was shoveling snow and has not improved with rest. He describes the pain as "crushing" and rates it a 9 out of 10 in severity. He reports associated nausea and diaphoresis but denies any shortness of breath or cough.

His medical history is significant for hypertension, Type 2 diabetes mellitus, and a 30-pack-year smoking history. Current medications include lisinopril and metformin. Physical examination reveals a patient in obvious distress, diaphoretic and clutching his chest. Blood pressure is 158/94 mmHg, heart rate is 104 bpm, respiratory rate is 20 breaths/min, and oxygen saturation is 96% on room air.

A 12-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) is performed and demonstrates 3-mm ST-segment elevation in leads V2 through V4. Cardiac biomarkers are pending.

Which of the following is the most likely pathophysiological mechanism responsible for this patient's clinical presentation?
